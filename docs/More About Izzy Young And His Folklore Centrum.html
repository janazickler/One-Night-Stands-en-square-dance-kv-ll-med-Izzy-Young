More About Izzy Young And His Folklore Center

Israel Goodman Young (1928-2019), widely known just as Izzy Young, was, and is still, considered one of the most important experts of folk music,
in the USA and in Sweden. In 1957 he opened The Folklore Center in Greenwich Village in New York, which was a type of music store with books, records
and magazines related to folk music. It was also a place where the concerts were held, and where folk and blues musicians from around the country would
come to gather and connect. There is a Bob Dylan’s song “Talkin’ Folklore Center” that describes Izzy’s store the best

“…On MacDougal Street I saw a cubby hole, I went in to get out of the cold, Found out after I’d entered, The place was called the Folklore Center
—Owned by Izzy Young —He’s always in back —Or the center. They got real records and real books, Anybody can walk in and look. You don’t have to own
a Cadillac car, Or a nine hundred and fifty-two dollar guitar —Do like most people do —Walk in —Walk around —Walk out…”

Izzy Young would let the still unknown, Bob Dylan, sit in the back of the store, reading books and listening to the records. Later on, Izzy produced
Dylan’s very first feature concert, in Carnegie Chapter Hall, NYC.

Besides Dylan, many other folk musicians were Izzy’s friends, as well as performers in The Folklore Center. Among them were many important and widely
known names such as Dave Van Ronk, Joni Mitchell and Tim Buckley, just to mention a few.

Izzy took a role in a political activism, as well. He was known as a leader of “the beatnik riot”, in 1961, where he led the march against the music
performance ban in Washington Square Park, Greenwich Village, NYC.

In the 1973, after he befriended two Swedish folk musicians and fell in love with Swedish folk music, Izzy closed his New York store and moved to
Stockholm, and opened the Folklore Centrum. The Folklore Centrum remained active till 2018. It had a unique collection of books, music records and CD’s,
as well as posters and photographs related to the history of folk music and folklore, as it also continued to be a place for meetings and concerts for
musicians and followers of the genre. The Folklore Centrum’s library, in its major part, is now hosted by the Bob Dylan Center in Tulsa, Oklahoma, and
Izzy’s personal writings and journals are housed at the Library of Congress in Washington D.C.
